Introduction to Floods in India

Floods in India are periodic and disastrous, every year many parts of the country get affected by Floods. According to the National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA), over 40 Mha ha of land in India is prone to flooding. The Ministry of Jal Shakti (MoJS) reported that the recent estimate on flood prone area in the country is 49.15 Mha, out of which Assam, Bihar, Odisha, Uttar Pradesh and West Bengal are largely affected. These floods have a severe impact on life, property, and infrastructure, to prevent and decrease the effects of Floods, it requires a combined effect from the government organizations, civil society, and individuals. The Indian government has taken measures to improve disaster management, early warning systems, and flood control infrastructure that include the construction of dams, embankments, and other flood control structures specifically to mitigate the impact of floods through different State & Central disaster management support organizations.

Cyclone Impact in India

The Indian subcontinent is one of the most cyclone-affected regions globally, particularly along the East coast. The majority of cyclones form in the Bay of Bengal, with about 70% making landfall on the East coast, affecting states like Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Tamil Nadu, and West Bengal. The region experiences an average of 5 to 6 cyclones per year, with 2 to 3 classified as severe.

Landslide Impact in India

India is among the top four countries with highest landslide risk. It has been estimated that loss of life per 100 km2 is greater than one. It accounts for about 8 per cent of global fatalities due to landslides.

Distribution of Reported Landslides in India

  • 66.5% in the Northwestern Himalayas
  • 18.8% in the Northeastern Himalayas
  • 14.7% in the Western Ghats

Government Schemes to mitigate Landslides

  • National Landslide Risk Management Strategy (2019): Comprehensive approach covering hazard mapping, monitoring, early warning systems, awareness, capacity building, policies, and stabilization measures.
  • Landslide Risk Mitigation Scheme (LRMS): Financial support for mitigation projects in vulnerable states, focusing on disaster prevention, early warning systems, and capacity building.
  • Flood Risk Mitigation Scheme (FRMS): Initiative for flood shelters, river basin-specific early warnings, and digital elevation maps for preparedness and evacuation.
  • Landslide Atlas of India: Detailed resource by NRSC (ISRO), documenting landslide events, assessments, and insights in vulnerable areas.
  • National Landslide Susceptibility Mapping (NLSM) Programme: Geological Survey of India (GSI) has completed landslide susceptibility mapping.
  • Indian Landslide Susceptibility Map (ILSM): IIT Delhi created the first high-resolution map for landslide susceptibility in India.
  • National Landslide Forecasting Centre (NLFC): Launched in Kolkata to establish a nationwide Landslide Early Warning System (LEWS) by 2030.

NDMA Guidelines for Landslide Disaster Management

  • NDMA Guidelines for Landslide Management: Comprehensive guidelines addressing hazard assessment, risk management, structural and non-structural measures, institutional mechanisms, financial arrangements, and community participation.
  • Landslide Hazard, Vulnerability & Risk Assessment: Identification of areas susceptible to landslides and assessment of at-risk resources.
  • Early Warning Systems for Landslides: Continuous monitoring of movements and stress development with regular data transmission for early warnings.
  • Investigations for Landslide Risk Assessment: Multi-disciplinary investigations to establish standards for mitigating landslide impacts.

Landslide: Do's and Don'ts

  • Do's:
    • Prepare tours to hilly regions based on weather or news reports.
    • Move away from landslide paths or downstream valleys quickly.
    • Keep drains clean and clear of debris (litter, leaves, etc.).
    • Grow trees to stabilize soil.
    • Identify areas of rock fall or cracks indicating landslides and move to safer areas.
    • Notice signals like muddy river waters indicating upstream landslides.
    • Contact nearest Tehsil or District Headquarters in case of danger.
    • Ensure slopes are not cut and trees are not uprooted unless re-vegetation is planned.
    • Listen for unusual sounds like cracking trees or rolling boulders.
    • Stay alert, awake, and active during possible impacts.
    • Locate and go to shelters, stay with family and companions.
    • Check for injured or trapped people.
    • Learn emergency communication signals for rescue teams.
  • Don'ts:
    • Avoid construction or staying in vulnerable areas.
    • Do not panic or waste energy.
    • Do not touch loose materials or electrical wiring.
    • Avoid building houses near steep slopes or drainage paths.
    • Do not drink contaminated water, but rainwater is safe if collected properly.
    • Do not move an injured person without proper first aid unless in immediate danger.
